# Negotiation Playbook

**Used:** Day 143-145 (offer phase)
**Goal:** Maximize compensation + flexibility without losing the offer
**Average outcome:** 10-25% improvement on initial offer

---

## Mindset (read this first)

1. **Negotiation is expected.** Not negotiating signals you don't value yourself. Companies bake in 10-25% room for negotiation in initial offers.
2. **Worst case = original offer.** They almost never rescind for negotiating professionally.
3. **Multiple offers = highest leverage.** Try to have 2-3 offers in flight simultaneously.
4. **Comp is more than salary.** Equity, signing bonus, learning budget, remote flexibility, vacation, equipment all negotiable.
5. **You are NOT being greedy.** They negotiated salary down — you negotiate it back up. That's the game.

---

## Pre-negotiation prep (do BEFORE first offer call)

- [ ] Research comp on **Levels.fyi** for the role/company/location
- [ ] Check **Blind** (Indian/US tech communities) for recent offers at that company
- [ ] Document your "BATNA" — Best Alternative To Negotiated Agreement (other offers, current salary, walk-away)
- [ ] Define 3 numbers for yourself:
  - **Floor:** Below this = walk away
  - **Target:** What you'd be happy with
  - **Stretch:** Asking number (10-20% above target)
- [ ] List non-salary asks (equity, sign-on, remote, etc.) prioritized

---

## Phase 1 — When recruiter says "we'd like to make an offer"

**DO NOT discuss numbers yet.** Get them to email the offer in writing.

### Reply
"That's wonderful, thank you! Could you send the full offer details in writing — base, equity, signing bonus, benefits, and start date? I'd like to review thoroughly before discussing."

### Why
- You can think clearly when not on a call
- Forces them to commit to a number on paper
- Buys 24-48 hrs

---

## Phase 2 — When you get the offer email

**Don't accept on the spot. Always counter.**

### Step 1 — Buy time (24-48 hrs)
"Thanks so much for this offer — really excited about [Company] and the team. I want to take 24-48 hours to review it fully and discuss with my partner. Can I get back to you by [day after tomorrow]?"

### Step 2 — Compare to your numbers
- Is base in your floor / target / stretch range?
- Is equity reasonable? (% of company × current valuation × vesting)
- Is signing bonus offered?
- Is location/remote acceptable?

### Step 3 — Decide your counter strategy

**If offer is at or above target:**
- Counter base by 5-10%, ask for sign-on bonus
- Ask for one non-cash thing (equipment budget, learning budget, extra week vacation)

**If offer is between floor and target:**
- Counter base by 15-20%
- Ask for sign-on bonus + equity bump
- Mention competing offer (real or "in flight")

**If offer is below floor:**
- Honestly: "The base is significantly below market for AI Engineer roles in my range — Levels.fyi shows median at $X for this role. Is there room to move base to $Y?"
- If they refuse: walk away

---

## Phase 3 — The counter call

**Schedule a call. Don't negotiate over email** (slower, higher misunderstanding risk).

### Script (starts after pleasantries)

"Thank you again for the offer — I'm very excited about the role, team, and product. I want to be transparent: I have [N] other offers/conversations in flight that are at higher comp bands. I'd love to make this work — would you be able to bring the base to $[target+10%], with the rest of the package as proposed?"

### If they push back on competing offers
- Be vague: "Two other companies in the AI fintech space" (don't name names)
- Don't lie if pressed: "I'd rather not name them, but happy to share company stage / size band if helpful"

### If they say "we're firm on base"
"Understood. Could we look at signing bonus or equity to bridge? A $[N] sign-on would help me make this work."

### If they say "let me check w/ leadership"
Great. Wait. Don't fill silence with "but I really want to work here!" — that signals desperation.

### If they come back with improved offer
**Always ask for one more thing.** Doesn't have to be salary:
- "Could we add [extra week vacation / $X learning budget / remote work flexibility]?"
- They almost always say yes to one small ask

### If they come back saying "best and final"
- Believe them.
- Ask for written confirmation of all terms.
- Take 24 hrs to decide.

## Phase 4 — Common objections + responses

### "We pay based on internal bands, not market data"
"I understand bands matter for parity. The market shifted significantly in AI roles in 2025-2026. Have these bands been updated recently? If they're 12+ months old, perhaps a reassessment is in order for AI Engineer roles specifically."

### "This is the highest we've ever offered for this level"
"That's good context — appreciate the transparency. Given my unique combination of [years of production .NET + 6 months shipped AI work + B2C product with revenue], I'd argue I'm not a typical candidate for this level. Could we explore promoting to next level [Senior / Staff]?"

### "We can't move on base but we can offer more equity"
- Calculate equity value at current valuation (likely already discounted in your head)
- Equity is risky — base pays bills
- Try: "I appreciate the equity offer. Could we do half the increase as base, half as equity? That balances risk for me."

### "If we increase your offer, our team will be unhappy"
"I understand and respect team parity. That's a problem worth solving with HR data — I'd be happy to share my Levels.fyi research after I sign, to support broader band updates."

### "You're already getting more than [other person at same level]"
"I respect that — and I'd argue different candidates bring different value. My recent 6-month shipping pace, B2C product traction, and YC application demonstrate uncommon execution speed. Comp should reflect contribution potential, not tenure."

### "Take it or leave it"
- Rare, usually a bluff
- Reply: "Could you give me 48 hrs to make sure this is the right move?"
- Use 48 hrs to push other offers
- If truly take-it-or-leave-it: take it if at floor, walk if below

## Phase 5 — Non-cash negotiables

If base is locked, push these:

| Item | Typical ask | Cost to company |
|------|-------------|-----------------|
| Signing bonus | $5K-25K | One-time, easy yes |
| Stock refresh | Extra grant year 1 | Zero cash, easy yes |
| Equipment budget | $3K-5K | One-time, easy yes |
| Annual learning | $2K-5K | Recurring, medium |
| Conference budget | $5K/yr | Easy yes |
| Extra vacation | 1-2 weeks | Easy yes |
| Remote work | Permanent | Sometimes hard |
| Earlier start | 1-2 weeks earlier | Easy if onboarding ready |
| Title bump | Senior → Staff | Hard but worth asking |
| Performance review accelerated | 6mo not 12mo | Easy yes |
| Relocation budget (if moving) | $10K-30K | Easy if relocating |
| Sign-on bonus | $5K-25K | Easy yes |

## Special situations

### Multiple offers in flight
Always disclose you have multiple offers, never reveal exact numbers from one to another. Use "competitive band" language: "My other offer is in the $X-Y range."

### Competing US remote vs Indian onsite offer
- US comp will dwarf India in absolute terms
- But India has lower cost of living + India market hire = stronger long-term resume in some cases
- Decide based on goals: max savings (US remote) vs network/learning (India GCC)

### Counter from a smaller company (no equity competition)
Push base harder. They can't compete on equity. Their leverage is mission/learning — make them pay for it.

### YC startup offer (low cash, high equity)
- Reasonable: $80-130K base, 0.5-2% equity
- Ask: "Could you increase base to $X — I have living expenses to cover during [N]-month transition"
- Equity at YC stage is usually fairly priced

### Relocation offer (you move to US/India)
- Always negotiate relocation package: $10-30K typical
- Ask for visa sponsorship details in writing
- Ask for temporary housing budget (1-2 months)

## Post-acceptance

- [ ] Get EVERYTHING in writing before signing
- [ ] Read offer letter carefully — non-compete, IP assignment, dispute resolution clauses
- [ ] Have a friend / lawyer review if equity > $50K value
- [ ] Sign, then announce on LinkedIn
- [ ] Send thank-you to recruiter + hiring manager
- [ ] Update `tracker/interviews/job-tracker.md` w/ final outcome

## Specific to your situation (Canadian → US/India)

### US remote (working from Canada)
- Most US companies hire via Deel/Remote.com (you're a contractor)
- Comp paid in USD, you handle Canadian taxes
- Negotiate for: USD-denominated comp, no salary cuts for FX
- Verify if they'll convert to W-2 if you move to US later (TN visa)

### Hyderabad GCC
- Comp in INR but often w/ US-equivalent bands
- Negotiate for: relocation if moving, joining bonus, retention bonus year 1
- ESOPs have lower upside than US startups (slow IPO route)
- Ask: "What's the salary review cycle? Can I get one at 9 months instead of 12?"

### Indian fintech startup
- Comp lower than GCCs in absolute INR
- Often offer ESOPs — verify cliff (usually 1 year), exercise window
- Negotiate for: title bump, fast salary review (6mo), remote flexibility

---

## Final advice

**Most candidates leave $10-50K on the table by not negotiating.** Over a 5-year career at one company, that's $50-250K. Easily worth the 30 min of awkward conversation.

**Your worst-case scenario** = the original offer.

**Your best-case scenario** = significantly higher comp + better terms + more respect from employer (companies value people who value themselves).

**Don't be greedy. Be informed and confident.**
